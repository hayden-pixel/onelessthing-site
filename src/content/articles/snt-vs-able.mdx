ABLE accounts and Special Needs Trusts (SNTs) solve the same core problem: how to hold money for a person with disabilities without disqualifying them from SSI (Supplemental Security Income) or Medicaid. Both are legitimate tools. Both work. But they're built for different situations, and choosing the wrong one can cost a family real money.

This guide explains how each works, where they differ, and which makes more sense depending on your circumstances.

## What an ABLE account is

An ABLE (Achieving a Better Life Experience) account is a tax-advantaged savings account available to people with disabilities that began before age 46. The account holder opens and controls it directly, much like a regular savings or investment account. Money in an ABLE account is excluded from the SSI resource limit up to $100,000.

Annual contributions are capped at $18,000 in 2026 (from all sources combined). The account holder can spend funds on qualified disability expenses, a broad category that includes housing, transportation, healthcare, education, and basic living expenses.

For a full overview, see [What Is an ABLE Account?](/articles/what-is-an-able-account).

## What a Special Needs Trust is

A Special Needs Trust (SNT) is a legal arrangement where assets are held by a trustee for the benefit of a person with a disability. Because the beneficiary doesn't directly own or control the assets, those assets are excluded from SSI and Medicaid resource calculations.

SNTs come in three main types:

**First-party (also called a d(4)(A) trust):** Funded with the disabled person's own money, typically from a personal injury settlement, inheritance received directly, or other lump sum. These trusts require Medicaid payback upon the beneficiary's death.

**Third-party trusts:** Funded by someone other than the beneficiary, typically parents, grandparents, or other family members. No Medicaid payback requirement. This is what most families set up through estate planning.

**Pooled trusts:** Managed by a nonprofit organization. Smaller accounts are combined for investment purposes while separate sub-accounts are maintained for each beneficiary. Often the most accessible option when a family doesn't have enough assets to justify a standalone trust.

## Key differences side by side

| | ABLE Account | Special Needs Trust |
|---|---|---|
| Who can open it | Account holder directly | Attorney (required) |
| Who controls spending | Account holder | Trustee |
| Annual contribution limit | $18,000 (2026) | No limit |
| Asset limit before SSI impact | $100,000 | No limit |
| Medicaid payback required | Yes (first-party funds) | Only for first-party trusts |
| Setup cost | Free to low | $2,000 to $5,000+ (attorney fees) |
| Ongoing administration | Account holder | Trustee (can be a family member) |
| Qualified expenses | Broad (defined by IRS) | Broad (defined by trustee) |

## When an ABLE account is the better choice

ABLE accounts are simpler, cheaper, and put the account holder in control. They make more sense when:

**The amounts are relatively modest.** If you're saving a few thousand dollars a year from a job or family contributions, an ABLE account is the right tool. There's no setup cost, no attorney required, and the account holder manages it directly.

**The account holder wants direct control.** With an SNT, a trustee makes spending decisions. With an ABLE account, the account holder spends the money, similar to a debit account. For many people, that independence matters.

**The money is for regular ongoing expenses.** ABLE accounts are designed for day-to-day use: rent, utilities, transportation, groceries. The account is set up for frequent transactions in a way that most trusts are not.

**The total amount won't exceed $100,000.** Above that threshold, SSI benefits are suspended. If you're managing amounts well below that, the ABLE account covers the need completely.

## When a Special Needs Trust is the better choice

SNTs handle situations that ABLE accounts can't. They make more sense when:

**A large sum of money is involved.** A personal injury settlement, a life insurance payout, or an inheritance can easily exceed the $100,000 ABLE threshold. A first-party SNT holds that money without any cap triggering SSI suspension.

**The family wants to leave money to a disabled loved one through an estate.** Parents and grandparents setting up wills or trusts should not leave money directly to a person on SSI. Direct inheritance counts as a resource and can eliminate benefits. A third-party SNT receives that money instead and holds it without triggering resource calculations.

**Avoiding Medicaid payback is a priority.** A third-party SNT has no Medicaid payback requirement. Whatever remains in the trust after the beneficiary's death passes to whoever the trust names as a remainder beneficiary. ABLE accounts, by contrast, are subject to Medicaid payback in most states.

**A trustee's oversight is actually helpful.** For some beneficiaries, having a responsible trustee manage distributions provides structure and protection that self-managed accounts can't offer.

## Can you have both?

Yes, and for many families, having both makes sense.

A common arrangement: the family funds a third-party SNT for large assets and estate planning purposes, while the beneficiary uses an ABLE account for everyday spending and smaller savings goals. The two tools work side by side without conflict.

One thing to note: funds transferred from a third-party SNT to an ABLE account count toward the ABLE annual contribution limit. Moving large amounts between the two in a single year requires planning.

## What this means for you

- If you're saving modest amounts and want direct control, start with an ABLE account. Setup is simple and free to low-cost.
- If a large sum of money is coming (settlement, inheritance), a first-party SNT is usually the right structure. Talk to a special needs attorney before accepting or spending those funds.
- If you're a parent doing estate planning, a third-party SNT is how you leave money to a disabled child without disrupting their benefits. Do not leave assets directly in a will without this protection in place.
- Having both an ABLE account and an SNT is common and often the right answer.
- Medicaid payback rules differ between the two tools. A third-party SNT has none. ABLE accounts and first-party SNTs both do.
